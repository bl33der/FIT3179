Smoking in Transition: Australia’s Changing Relationship with Tobacco

Smoking remains one of the leading preventable causes of death worldwide, yet Australia stands out for its long-term decline in tobacco use. This visual story explores the changing patterns of smoking across the country: by state, gender, age, and community, and compares them globally. Through these data-driven insights, we uncover where progress has been greatest, which groups remain at risk, and how Australia’s tobacco control policies continue to shape healthier futures.

National overview

Smoking share by state (%)

The data shows that smoking prevalence is not evenly distributed across Australia. Tasmania and the Northern Territory have notably higher rates, whereas ACT and Victoria sit well below the national mean. These gaps highlight the geographic dimension of smoking inequality — a key factor for policy targeting.

The burden isn’t uniform. High-prevalence states are where prevention funding and awareness campaigns could yield the greatest marginal impact.

Source: Australian Bureau of Statistics

Demographic patterns

Smoking trend by gender

While men continue to smoke more than women, the gap between genders has gradually narrowed — a sign that anti-smoking efforts are resonating across the population. This convergence suggests social change, improved education, and equal access to cessation support.

The gender gap in smoking is closing, reflecting a broader cultural shift in health attitudes.

Source: Australian Bureau of Statistics

Smokers by age group

The grouped bar chart compares smoking levels across age groups and survey years. It reveals that younger Australians (particularly those aged 15–24) have experienced the steepest decline in smoking.

The data indicates a generational transition: new cohorts are entering adulthood with lower smoking rates than ever before. Meanwhile, older groups (45+) show slower declines, implying entrenched habits.

Source: Australian Bureau of Statistics

Origin and community

Smokers in Australia by country of origin

This donut chart visualises the share of smokers by country of origin. Australian-born residents represent the largest group, but migrant communities show varied smoking rates, reflecting different cultural and socioeconomic influences.

Some overseas-born groups have higher or lower smoking rates depending on their country of origin and access to public health messaging. These variations underscore the importance of culturally specific tobacco control measures

Source: data/SMOKER_BY_ETHNICITY.csv

Global context

Global smoking prevalence (2025)

Australia ranks among the lowest in global smoking prevalence, aligning with countries like Canada and Norway. In contrast, many regions in Eastern Europe and Southeast Asia maintain significantly higher smoking rates.

Australia’s sustained policy and education efforts place it as a global leader in tobacco control — though continued vigilance is necessary to maintain progress.
